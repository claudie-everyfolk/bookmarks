# The Hidden Cost of AI Tab-Switching: 23 Minutes Per Context Switch

**Author:** Aakash Gupta (@aakashgupta)
**Tweet:** https://x.com/aakashgupta/status/2057460415479919074
**Date:** 2026-05-21

> Gloria Mark at UC Irvine measured this for two decades. A single context switch takes an average of 23 minutes and 15 seconds to return to the same depth of focus. Along the way, you touch 2.3 unrelated tasks before you get back.
>
> Run that against 20 to 30 AI tab switches a day. You leave your editor, open Claude, describe what you're looking at, read the answer, go back, try to find your place... By the fifth switch, you've stopped returning to baseline. You're running on a degraded version of your own attention for the rest of the session.
>
> The questions you don't ask because the switching cost feels too high are the ones that would have caught the bug, fixed the export setting, or saved the reshoot.

## Why it's relevant

The strongest argument yet for *where* AI should live: in the workflow, not in a separate tab. Gupta's point — backed by two decades of attention research — is that AI-in-a-tab quietly degrades focus, and the real loss is the questions people *don't* ask. This is directly useful for Every's training framing: the goal isn't "use AI more," it's "remove the context-switch tax" — embed AI where the work already happens (Claude Code in the editor, voice interfaces, agents that run in the background). It's also a quiet validation of how Claudie is set up: scheduled jobs and Slack-native access mean the team doesn't tab-switch *to* Claudie — she shows up inside their existing surface. Good material for Mike and Brooker when explaining why tool placement matters as much as tool capability.
